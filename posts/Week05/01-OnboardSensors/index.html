Overview

iOS devices come with a suite of onboard sensors, but keep in mind not all devices come with all sensors. Some of those sensors are:

  • The accelerometer, which gives directional data describing device motion.
  • The gyroscope, which gives data describing the devices orientation in 3 dimensional space.
  • The ambient light sensor, which has no public API, but provides the device with a light level with which it can adjust the brightness of the screen.
  • The proximity sensor, which sits near the front facing camera, which tells the device whether it is up against your face.
  • Assisted GPS, which is GPS + Wifi - which of course helps determine your location on the planet.
  • A barometer to measure changes in air pressure, but doesn't provide actual values.
  • An altimeter to help determine your altitude (which is typically used to determine the floor of a building you may be on).

The true depth camera was added to the list of available sensors with the introduction of the iPhone X. The addition of an infrared camera, a flood illuminator and a dot projector allows for body and face detection, bringing FaceID and other related technologies to life.

In addition to getting information from the onboard sensors, you can also simulate certain actions the device may undergo during use:

  • Rotate left and right change the device from portrait to landscape mode (and back).
  • You can simulate the user shaking the device.
  • Pressing the home button.
  • Locking the device.
  • Rebooting device.
  • You can simulate a user location via some preset locations, or you can enter your own latitude and longitude.

Finally, you can simulate your device’s display to external screens. First, you can send your display to an external Airplay screen. Airplay is the Apple protocol that allows you to send audio and video over to supported external devices. You can also simulate your app’s display on an apple watch, or or an Apple TV.
